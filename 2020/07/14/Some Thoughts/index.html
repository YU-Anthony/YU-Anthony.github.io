Intention

In real life, different drivers have different driving behaviors: for example, some drivers will consciously "help" vehicles that want to get on the ramp, while others will not. Therefore, it is difficult for unmanned vehicles to interact with vehicles with arbitrary driving behaviors on the road. Our goal is to find out the driving scenarios where drivers will produce different driving behaviors, so as to further study how to make automatic cars produce adaptive behaviors to enhance the user experience.

2. Adaptive Strategies under Merging Scenarios

Self-driving cars in the market often adopt the most conservative driving strategies when encountering merging scenarios. If a car wants to get on the ramp, it will stop until there is no car on another road or other cars are very far away from it. Even if the road priority of the two intersections are the same, the current unmanned vehicle system will also choose to avoid other vehicles until it thinks it is safe enough to move on. Obviously, this driving strategy is totally inapplicable under the condition of dense traffic flow, and it is very difficult to get on the ramp successfully if it is completely dependent on the current unmanned vehicle system.

Hu yeping 2 thought that the decision making during merging scenario is mainly based on the road priorities and she divided it into 4 subclass: (a) Higher priority (b) Lower priority (c) Equal priority (d) Freeway merging.

Figure 2. Illustrations of various merging scenarios. Red car represents the autonomous vehicle. In (a) and (b), cars with higher priorities have the right of way. However, some level of negotiations is necessary if the traffic gets dense. In (c), the car which is closer to the merge point has the right of way. However, negotiation is required since sometime it is difficult to define who is closer and each driver may have different opinions. Different from other scenarios, the red car in (d) has unknown merge point during merging.

3. TraficNet

TrafficNet 3 is a large-scale and extensible library of naturalistic driving scenarios which, in total consist of 565,291 events.

Figure 3. TrafficNet Overview

It integrates the extraction of 6 critical scenarios in the current stage, including free flow, car-following, lane change, frontal cut-in pedestrian crossing, and cyclist.

Summary

In summary, we may consider to design the scenarios from the following aspects.

  • Free follow
  • Car following
  • Lane change
  • Adjacent car cut out
  • Front car cut in
  • Pedestrian crossing
  • Cyclist
  • Stop ○ Traffic light stop ○ Stop sign
  • Merging ○ Higher priority ○ Lower priority ○ Equal priority ○ Freeway merging
Free Flow

Free flow is the scenario where a vehicle goes freely without being blocked by other vehicles in front of it.

Car following

Car following contains the events where a vehicle keeps following one car without changing its lane.

Lane Change

In a lane change event, the vehicle changes from one lane to another. The lane change maneuver exposures the vehicle under the possibility of conflict and crash.

Pedestrain Crossing

Pedestrain Crossing logged the information on events when a pedestrian crosses the street in front of the vehicle

Cyclist

Cyclist records the positions, in terms of longitude and latitude, of a vehicle and a cyclist in time sequence when they encounter each other.

Front car cut-in

Front car cut-in scenario is a situation in which a vehicle in front of the ego-vehicle performs a lane change into the lane of the ego-vehicle at a distance which can be deemed unsafe.

Adjacent car cut out

Adjacent car cut out is a car in front leaves the lane abruptly to avoid a stopped vehicle ahead

Traffic light stop

The driver in this situation makes the decision to brake and stop or to proceed through the intersection.

Stop sign

We can study the braking measures after the driver sees stop sign.
